Replication of Karlan and List (2007)

Does Price Matter in Charitable Giving? Evidence from a Large-Scale Natural Field Experiment

Introduction

Karlan and List (2007) conducted one of the most influential natural field experiments in the economics of charitable giving. The authors partnered with a liberal US nonprofit organization to send direct mail solicitations to over 50,000 prior donors. The central question is whether price — as manipulated through matching grants — affects both the likelihood and amount of charitable giving.

The experimental design randomly assigned donors to a control group (no match offer) or one of several treatment groups varying along three dimensions: (1) the match ratio ($1:$1, $2:$1, or $3:$1), (2) the maximum size of the matching grant ($25,000, $50,000, $100,000, or unstated), and (3) the suggested donation amount (equal to, 1.25×, or 1.5× the donor’s highest previous contribution).

The paper’s headline findings are:

  • Simply announcing a matching grant increases response rates by ~22% and revenue per solicitation by ~19%.
  • Contrary to conventional fundraising wisdom, larger match ratios (2:1, 3:1) have no additional effect over a 1:1 match.
  • Treatment effects are heterogeneous by political environment: the match worked strongly in “red” states but had little effect in “blue” states.

These findings challenge the common fundraising belief that bigger matches attract bigger responses, and raise interesting questions about the role of political identity in charitable behavior.

Table 1 — Balance Check

Table 1 confirms that randomization produced balanced treatment and control groups across all observable pre-treatment characteristics.

rows = []
for label, var in balance_vars.items():
    if var not in df.columns:
        continue
    trt_vals  = df.loc[df["treatment"] == 1, var].dropna()
    ctrl_vals = df.loc[df["treatment"] == 0, var].dropna()
    pval      = stats.ttest_ind(trt_vals, ctrl_vals).pvalue
    rows.append({
        "Variable":  label,
        "All":       round(df[var].mean(), 3),
        "Treatment": round(trt_vals.mean(), 3),
        "Control":   round(ctrl_vals.mean(), 3),
        "p-value":   round(pval, 3),
    })

balance_df = pd.DataFrame(rows)

Table 3 — Regression Results (Likelihood of Giving)

The paper runs probit models but reports coefficients that closely match OLS (a known quirk). We use OLS (linear probability model), which replicates the paper’s numbers.

Something Additional: Visualizing the Red/Blue State Heterogeneity

One of the paper’s most striking findings is that the match effect is entirely concentrated in red states. The paper shows this only in regression tables and a small scatter plot — here we visualize it as a clear bar chart.

Code 
summary = (
    df.groupby(["red0", "treatment"])["gave"]
    .agg(mean="mean", sem="sem")
    .reset_index()
)
summary["red_label"] = summary["red0"].map({0: "Blue State", 1: "Red State"})
summary["group"]     = summary["treatment"].map({0: "Control", 1: "Treatment\n(Match Offer)"})

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))

colors    = {"Control": "#888888", "Treatment\n(Match Offer)": "#2a6ebb"}
x_pos     = {"Blue State": 0, "Red State": 1}
offsets   = {"Control": -0.15, "Treatment\n(Match Offer)": 0.15}
seen      = set()

for _, row in summary.iterrows():
    x     = x_pos[row["red_label"]] + offsets[row["group"]]
    label = row["group"] if row["group"] not in seen else "_nolegend_"
    seen.add(row["group"])
    ax.bar(x, row["mean"], width=0.25,
           color=colors[row["group"]], label=label, alpha=0.85, edgecolor="white")
    ax.errorbar(x, row["mean"], yerr=1.96 * row["sem"],
                fmt="none", color="black", capsize=4, linewidth=1.2)

ax.set_xticks([0, 1])
ax.set_xticklabels(["Blue State", "Red State"], fontsize=12)
ax.yaxis.set_major_formatter(mticker.PercentFormatter(xmax=1, decimals=1))
ax.set_ylabel("Response Rate", fontsize=11)
ax.set_title("Match Offer Effect on Donation Rate:\nRed vs. Blue States",
             fontsize=13, fontweight="bold")
ax.legend(title="Group", fontsize=10)
ax.spines[["top", "right"]].set_visible(False)
plt.tight_layout()

This is puzzling from a pure price perspective (the match lowers the cost of giving equally everywhere) and suggests that political identity, social signaling, or minority-group dynamics play a key role in donor responsiveness.

Conclusion

This replication confirms all three main findings of Karlan and List (2007):

  1. Matching grants work — announcing a match significantly increases participation and revenue.
  2. Match ratio doesn’t matter — 2:1 and 3:1 perform identically to 1:1, refuting conventional fundraising wisdom.
  3. Context matters enormously — the entire effect is driven by red-state donors, pointing to non-price factors like political identity.

Causal inference is valid here because of random assignment: the balance table confirms groups were equivalent on all pre-treatment observables, so any difference in outcomes is attributable to the match offer itself — not to who received it.
